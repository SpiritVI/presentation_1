Learning to doubt in order to approach the truth

References :

  1. senses (Latin term) : faculty or power of perceiving; perception, feeling, sensation, sense
    • Also call : induction, reasoning that consists of moving from the particular to the universal.

  2. ratio (Latin term) : reason
    • Also called : deduction, the inverse of induction, involves deducing new propositions from axioms and proven properties.
    Exemple 1: Manilius says : they observed the appearance of the whole night sky and watched every star return to its original place […] by doing this repeatedly, they built up their knowledge.
    Exemple 2: The Aetna is an anonymous text that explains how volcanoes work. Its unknown author warns readers about two potential sources of misinformation: other authors and other people.
    Selected quotes to summarize each lesson:
    • Lesson 1 : "Doubting everything or believing everything: these are two equally convenient strategies, both of which spare us from having to think." - Henri Poincaré
    • Lesson 2 : "To err is human, to forgive, divin." - Alexander Pope.

Exemple 3:

In On the Nature of Things, Roman philosopher Lucretius proposed three different explanations for solar eclipses:

  • the Moon passing in front of the Sun
  • some other opaque body passing in front of the Sun, or
  • the Sun’s light temporarily growing dim for some reason.
