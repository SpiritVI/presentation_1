When Thales meets IA

Learning to doubt in order to approach the truth

Lexicon :

  1. "Senses" (in Antiquity) : faculty or power of perceiving; perception, feeling, sensation, sense
  2. "Ratio" (in Antiquity) : reason

Nowadays, we call this reasonning by induction and deduction. The first one consists of reflecting from particular to the universal, the second one is the opposite.

Exemple 1: "Manilius says : they observed the appearance of the whole night sky and watched every star return to its original place […] by doing this repeatedly, they built up their knowledge."
Exemple 2: "The Aetna is an anonymous text that explains how volcanoes work. Its unknown author warns readers about two potential sources of misinformation: other authors and other people."

Some quotes to illustrate:
Lesson 1 : "Doubting everything or believing everything: these are two equally convenient strategies, both of which spare us from having to think." - Henri Poincaré
Lesson 2 : "To err is human, to forgive, divin." - Alexander Pope.

The courage to doubt

Exemple 3: "In On the Nature of Things, Roman philosopher Lucretius proposed three different explanations for solar eclipses:

  1. the Moon passing in front of the Sun
  2. some other opaque body passing in front of the Sun, or
  3. the Sun’s light temporarily growing dim for some reason."

Some quotes to illustrate:
Lesson 3:"What we know is a drop in the ocean; what we don't know is an ocean." - Isaac Newton

Curiosity & collective progress

Exemple 4: "An ancient medical text from the school of Hippocrates called On the Sacred Disease sought to explain the causes of epilepsy."
Exemple 5: "The anonymous Aetna author says something similar: “Science is no place for genius.”"

Some quotes to illustrate:
Lesson 4 : "It is better to accept what we cannot prove than to pretend we know what we do not." - Lucretius
Lesson 5 : "Curiosity is the first step towards truth." - Louis Pasteur
